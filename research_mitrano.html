Mitrano Lab

THz Emission and Detection

During my time in the Mitrano Lab, I was responsible for multiple small projects, culminating in the creation and alignment of a transient-grating frequency resolved optical gating (TG FROG). However, my primary role was to work on an independent project: a closed loop terahertz (THz) emission and detection system using photoconductive antennas (PCAs). This project required research, the design of an optical setup, development of a coding interface to talk to the equipment and general data collection. At first appearing straightforward, I had to make many adjustments to my setup along the way, even developing an integrated microscope system to aid in alignment. In January of 2023, I passed the project onto a new undergraduate student who is still working on the project now.

Photoconductive Antenna

A PCA is a metal diode patterned into a photoconductive semiconductor substrate. An external DC voltage is applied to the emitter PCA and therefore when the laser is incident on the antenna gap, a circuit is completed. By pulsing the laser at ultra high frequencies, we can generate a transient photocurrent that is reemitted as a THz pulse. A THz pulse can also be detected by a PCA that is not biased. For the receiver PCA, when an incident THz pulse hits the gap, it drives some current, but this is only possible when the laser is also incident on the PCA completing the gap. This means that the pulsed laser and pulsed THz have to arrive at the receiver PCA at exactly the same time. This is what makes the setup so tricky.

Microscope

One of the primary challenges I encountered was being able to align the beam precisely on the PCA gap. The gap is only 5 micrometers and being off by even a little would result in a weak or more likely no signal. To be able to get this precision, I developed an integrated microscope into my optical design. This started out with a simple proof of concept using a white light source, a lens and an index card. It was honestly so exciting that such a simple set-up could result in such a sharply magnified image.

Digital Microscope

By using two beam splitters and a camera (rather than an index card), I was able to build a microscope directly into the setup. This microscope would allow me to clearly align the beam on the tiny PCA gap.

Alignment

To align the beam on the PCA gap, I just had to wait until I saw a large diffraction pattern generated from the beam reflecting off the gold diode on either side of the gap. When I had a strong diffraction pattern, I knew the beam was aligned.

Final Setup

This diagram displays the final optical setup that I created. Importantly, notice the two mirrors on the translation stage. By progressing the stage forward and back, we are able to change the length of the red laser beam path. In order to collect a signal, we need the red laser beam path to be the same length as the orange path plus the distance of the THz pulse.

Scanning

To scan for a signal I created a coding interface to be able to control the actuator remotely and collect data points from the lock-in amplifier (that is connected to the receiver PCA). Ultimately, I left the project before a confirmed signal was established, but the project was now ready to test novel devices.
